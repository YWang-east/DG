Introduction to DG#

The Discontinuous Galerkin Method#

Consider the 1D scalar conservation law

\[\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial f(u)}{\partial x} = 0.\]

To solve the above equation, the idea is to discretize the physical domain into K non-overlapping cells \(D^k\). The local solution within the cell is assumed to be

\[x \in D^k=[x_l^k, \ x_r^k]: \ u_h^k(x) = \sum_{i=1}^{N_p} u_h^k(x_i^k) \ell_i^k(x),\]

where \(x_i^k\) are the \(N_p\) distinct nodes (or grid points) and \(\ell_i^k(x)\) are polynomials of degree \(N_p - 1\).

Multiplying the conservation law by a test function \(\ell_j^k(x)\) and integrating over the cell \(D^k\) lead to

\[\mathcal{M}^k \frac{d}{d t} \boldsymbol{u}_h^k+\mathcal{S}^k\boldsymbol{f}_h^k =\left[\ell^k(x) \left(f_h^k - f_h^*\right)\right]_{x_l^k}^{x_r^k},\]

with

  • the solution vector \(\boldsymbol{u}_h^k = [u_1^k, \dots, u_{N_p}^k]^T\),

  • the flux vector \(\boldsymbol{f}_h^k = [f_1^k, \dots, f_{N_p}^k]^T\),

  • the numerical flux \(f_h^* = f(u_h^*)\),

  • the mass and stiffness matrices

\[\mathcal{M}^k = \int_{D^k} \ell_i^k(x) \ell_j^k(x) dx, \quad \mathcal{S}^k = \int_{D^k} \ell_i^k(x) \frac{d \ell_j^k(x)}{dx} dx.\]

Unlike the conventional finite element method (FEM), solutions can be discontinuous at cell interfaces. They are coupled through the numerical flux \(f_h^*\), which recovers the global solution. This property resembles the finite volume method (FVM), but the DG method can easily achieve high-order accuracy by simply increasing the local polynomial degree.

Legendre-Gauss-Lobatto quadrature#

Consider a reference cell \(D^{ref} = [-1, \ 1]\), where the local solution can be approximated by

\[r\in D^{ref}: \ u_h(r) = \sum_{i=1}^{N_p} u(r_i) \ell_i(r),\]

with the Lagrange polynomial basis

\[\begin{split}\ell_i(r) = \prod_{\substack{j=1 \\ j \neq i}}^{N_p} \frac{r-r_j}{r_i-r_j}.\end{split}\]

Here, \(r_i\) are the Legendre-Gauss-Lobatto quadrature nodes.

The mass and stiffness matrices in the reference cell can be computed as

\[\mathcal{M} = (\mathcal{V} \mathcal{V}^T)^{-1}, \quad \mathcal{S} = \mathcal{M} \mathcal{D},\]

where the Vandermonde matrix \(\mathcal{V}\) and the differentiation matrix \(\mathcal{D}\) are

\[\mathcal{V}_{ij} = \tilde{P}_j(r_i), \quad \mathcal{V}_{r, (ij)} = \frac{d \tilde{P}_j}{d r} \Bigg|_{r_i}, \quad \mathcal{D} = \mathcal{V}_r \mathcal{V}^{-1}\]

with \(\tilde{P}_j(r)\) the Legendre polynomial.

Cell-wise operations#

The reference cell can be mapped to a generic cell \(D^k = [x_l^k, \ x_r^k]\) through the mapping

\[x(r) = x_l^k + \frac{1+r}{2}(x_r^k - x_l^k).\]

The mass and stiffness matrices after the mapping become

\[\mathcal{M}^k = \frac{\Delta x}{2} \mathcal{M}, \quad \mathcal{S}^k = \mathcal{M} \mathcal{D}\]

Finally the DG scheme can be written as

\[\frac{d \boldsymbol{u}^k}{dt} = \frac{2}{\Delta x} \left( - \mathcal{D} \boldsymbol{f}^k + \mathcal{M}^{-1} \mathcal{B} (\boldsymbol{f}^k - \boldsymbol{f}^*) \right)\]

with boundary matrix \(\mathcal{B} = \textrm{diag}(-1,0,\dots,0,1)\)

Numerical flux#

The implemented numerical flux solvers are

Lax-Friedrichs flux#

\[f^* = \frac{f(u^+) + f(u^-)}{2} - \frac{\alpha}{2}(u^+ - u^-),\]

where \(u^+\) and \(u^-\) are the right and left nodal values across a cell interface respectively, and

\[\alpha = \max |f'(u)|.\]

Time Integration#

Consider the semi-discrete equation

\[\frac{du}{dt} = L(u),\]

where operator :math::L stands for the resultant spatial discretization from the DG method. Currently, The implemented time integration methods are

2nd order Runge-Kutta (RK2)#

\[\begin{split}u^{(1)} = u^n + \frac{\Delta t}{2} L(u^n) \\ u^{n+1} = u^{(1)} + \Delta t L(u^{(1)})\end{split}\]

Strong stability preserving 3rd order Runge-Kutta (SSP_RK3)#

\[\begin{split}u^{(1)} &= u^n + \Delta t L(u^n) \\ u^{(2)} &= \frac{3}{4}u^n + \frac{1}{4}u^{(1)} + \frac{\Delta t}{4} L(u^{(1)}) \\ u^{n+1} &= \frac{1}{3}u^n + \frac{2}{3}u^{(2)} + \frac{2\Delta t}{3} L(u^{(2)})\end{split}\]
